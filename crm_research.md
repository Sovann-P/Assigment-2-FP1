# What is CRM?

CRM is also known as Customer Relationship Management. CRM is essentially the different strategies, processes, and technologies that many organizations use in order to manage and analyze interactions with customers that they have or future customers.

The purpose of CRM is to improve business relationships and aims to do many other things. Some other goals is centralizing customer data, streamlining customer service processes, increasing revenue through better lead tracking, improve customer retention, and enable data-driven decision making. These are just some of the business goals. For the customer, CRM aims to experience faster issue resolution for customers, give customers a more personalized service and communication, and provide consistent experience throughout different aspects such as email, phone, online, etc.

The history of CRM goes a good way back. Before the 1980s, businesses would rely on things like paper files and handwritten ledgers to track various things like customer info. Relationships were also managed manually and were often dependent on individual salespeople's memory and notebooks. However, during the 1980s, database marketing started to arise which allowed companies to store and query customer data digitally. Moving on to the early 1990s, Sales Force Automation emerged. This had software that automated repetitive sales tasks. The late 1990s came which then created the term CRM which was widely adopted. In 1999, Salesforce.com launched which pioneered the SaaS model which delivered CRM all over the internet.

Evolution of CRM had many different phases. Early CRM focused on automating front-office processes such as customer support ticketing. Then Analytical CRM came as data grew and CRM systems started incorporating analytics. It then kept evolving and came social and mobile CRM. This was when the rise of smartphones and social media pushed CRM to do things like tracking interactions on various social media platforms. Now in the present, we have AI-powered CRM. Many CRM platforms now have embed AI for doing tasks such as automated follow-ups.

# Why do companies use CRM systems?

Companies use CRM systems because of reasons such as sales management, customer management, marketing, customer support, and reporting and analytics. For sales management, CRM gives sales teams more structure and speed which shortens sales cycles. In customer management, every interaction such as calls, emails, etc. are stored in one profile. This makes it much easier for an employee of a company to see the history of a customer relationship. In marketing, CRM makes marketing more personalized and more directly tied to business outcomes. Some strategies that it uses are marketing automation, targeted campaigns, and more. Another popular reason why companies use CRM is because of customer support. CRM gives different support teams the context, tools, and data that they may need in order to resolve issues much faster. Companies also use CRM because of reporting and analytics. CRM transforms raw customer data into something more strategic which enables organizations to make smarter, faster, and evidence-based decisions.

# What kind of business problems do CRM systems solve?

CRM systems solve many different business problems especially from healthcare, retail, nonprofits, manufacturing, and education. Across all of these different industries, CRM is what allows them to manage relationships and information about the people that use their business. While they are all different, the goal is ultimately to organize data, make better decisions, and improve communication.

# What are the major modules found in a CRM?

Many CRM systems are built around a couple of different major modules. These modules help organizations manage things like customers and business processes. One module is contacts and that essentially stores different information about customers and organizations. Another module is accounts which stores things such as company name, website, revenue, etc. Customer support tickets is also another module which stores things like ticket numbers and customers that are linked to the ticket.
