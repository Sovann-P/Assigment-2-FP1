## Part 1: Understanding CRM

### What is CRM?

CRM is also known as Customer Relationship Management. CRM is essentially the different strategies, processes, and technologies that many organizations use in order to manage and analyze interactions with customers that they have or future customers.

The purpose of CRM is to improve business relationships and aims to do many other things. Some other goals is centralizing customer data, streamlining customer service processes, increasing revenue through better lead tracking, improve customer retention, and enable data-driven decision making. These are just some of the business goals. For the customer, CRM aims to experience faster issue resolution for customers, give customers a more personalized service and communication, and provide consistent experience throughout different aspects such as email, phone, online, etc.

### History of CRM

The history of CRM goes a good way back. Before the 1980s, businesses would rely on things like paper files and handwritten ledgers to track various things like customer info. Relationships were also managed manually and were often dependent on individual salespeople's memory and notebooks. However, during the 1980s, database marketing started to arise which allowed companies to store and query customer data digitally. Moving on to the early 1990s, Sales Force Automation emerged. This had software that automated repetitive sales tasks. The late 1990s came which then created the term CRM which was widely adopted. In 1999, Salesforce.com launched which pioneered the SaaS model which delivered CRM all over the internet.

### Evolution of CRM

Evolution of CRM had many different phases. Early CRM focused on automating frontoffice processes such as customer support ticketing. Then Analytical CRM came as data grew and CRM systems started incorporating analytics. It then kept evolving and came social and mobile CRM. This was when the rise of smartphones and social media pushed CRM to do things like tracking interactions on various social media platforms. Now in the present, we have AI-powered CRM. Many CRM platforms now have embed AI for doing tasks such as automated follow-ups.

### Why Do Companies Use CRM Systems?

Companies use CRM systems because of reasons such as sales management, customer management, marketing, customer support, and reporting and analytics.

- **Sales Management** — CRM gives sales teams more structure and speed which shortens sales cycles.
- **Customer Management** — Every interaction such as calls, emails, etc. are stored in one profile. This makes it much easier for an employee of a company to see the history of a customer relationship.
- **Marketing** — CRM makes marketing more personalized and more directly tied to business outcomes. Some strategies that it uses are marketing automation, targeted campaigns, and more. Another popular reason why companies use CRM is because of customer support.
- **Customer Support** — CRM gives different support teams the context, tools, and data that they may need in order to resolve issues much faster. Companies also use CRM because of reporting and analytics.
- **Reporting and Analytics** — CRM transforms raw customer data into something more strategic which enables organizations to make smarter, faster, and evidence-based decisions.

### What Kind of Business Problems Do CRM Systems Solve?

CRM systems solve many different business problems especially from healthcare, retail, nonprofits, manufacturing, and education. Across all of these different industries, CRM is what allows them to manage relationships and information about the people that use their business. While they are all different, the goal is ultimately to organize data, make better decisions, and improve communication.

### What Are the Major Modules Found in a CRM?

Many CRM systems are built around a couple of different major modules. These modules help organizations manage things like customers and business processes.

- **Contacts** — Essentially stores different information about customers and organizations.
- **Accounts** — Stores things such as company name, website, revenue, etc.
- **Customer Support Tickets** — Stores things like ticket numbers and customers that are linked to the ticket.

## Part 2: Commercial and Open Source CRM Comparison

### Commercial CRM Products

| Product | Target Customer | Strengths | Weaknesses | Pricing Model |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Salesforce CRM** | Medium to large businesses | Salesforce is very customizable and has many third-party integrations. They also have automation and powerful analytics. | Salesforce is expensive and implementing it could get complex. | It is a subscription based CRM with multiple different tiers. |
| **HubSpot CRM** | Small to medium businesses or startups | It is easy to use and has a free option as well. They also have quick deployment and powerful marketing/sales integration. | It's not as customizable, and the more complex features require a much higher priced plan. | They have a free model available as well as paid sales, marketing, and service hub subscriptions. |
| **Zoho CRM** | Small to medium businesses | Zoho is affordable and has many different feature sets. They also have an AI assistant. | The UI is less impressive as other CRMs and they have less advanced capabilities. | It is subscription based. |
| **Microsoft Dynamics 365 CRM** | Medium to large businesses with an emphasis on Microsoft users | They have great integration with Microsoft 365, Power BI, and Azure. They also have strong analytics and automation and is very scalable. | This CRM can be complex to configure and it has a high cost for advanced modules. | It has modular subscription pricing. Businesses pay for selected applications and users. |

### Analysis

Out of the four CRM Systems that I have researched, Salesforce is the most popular. One reason being that Salesforce holds the largest share of the global CRM market. It is also used by over 150,000 businesses worldwide, ranging from both small and large businesses. It also has the largest CRM ecosystem and is extremely customizable. Finally, it is also viewed as the industry standard for CRM Systems.

Out of the three open source CRM systems that I have researched, SuiteCRM stands out as the most mature. This is because it has had over a decade of continuous development. It also has a huge ecosystem of extensions, consultants, and also community contributors.

Out of all of these CRM systems, the one I would recommend for a smaller business is HubSpot CRM. This is because it is much easier to use than the others and it also has a free plan available. Because it is less complex, you won't need extensive training to use it for your employees. It is also very scalable, so you can pay to get more features later as your business grows.

Out of all of these CRM systems, the one I would recommend for a bigger enterprise is Salesforce. Like we discussed earlier, Salesforce is the leading global CRM system. It is used by many businesses around the world and has extremely high customizability and features.
